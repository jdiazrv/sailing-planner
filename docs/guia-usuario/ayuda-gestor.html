Manager help

Sailing Planner at a glance

Sailing Planner helps you organize boat operations across a season. As a manager, your usual work is to prepare the boat, outline the route in segments, register visits, block unavailable periods, and grant the right access to the right people.

This guide is intended for managers and operational editors, not superusers.

Purpose

What the application solves

The app centralizes what usually ends up scattered across messages, loose notes, and memory: the boat plan, the route sequence, who boards and leaves, which days remain free, and which periods must stay untouched.

Single source of truth

Everything revolves around one active season and one shared timeline so the team sees the same plan.

Less friction

Segments, visits, and blocked periods are cross-checked, so you can detect gaps, overlaps, or missing data before operating.

Normal flow

Recommended working order

1

Configure the boat

Review the display name, model, year, home port, and image. This gives the rest of the team context and avoids incomplete dashboards.

2

Create the season

The season defines the full operating time frame. Without it there is no coherent timeline or planning.

3

Add route segments

Each segment represents a route stage or stop, with dates, place, and status. Start with the overall structure and refine it later.

4

Register visits

Record who boards, when they board, when they leave, and from where. Visits complete the operational picture of the season.

5

Block unavailable periods

Use blocked periods for maintenance, cleaning, handovers, or non-operational days. This prevents the calculated availability from showing a false free gap.

6

Share with the right permission level

Invite readers if they only need to consult, and create editors when they are responsible for maintaining boat, route, or visit data.

Concepts

What each part means

Boat

The base asset record. Everything else hangs from here.

Season

The navigation period that structures the whole timeline.

Segment or stop

A part of the route with dates, location, and status. It defines the actual trip path.

Visit

A guest or crew stay with boarding and disembark dates.

Blocked period

A non-operational period that must be excluded from availability.

Timeline

The view that combines route, visits, and availability so you can detect inconsistencies quickly.

Access

When to use invites and when to create editors

Read-only invite

Use it when someone only needs to review the season, understand the plan, or follow visits without editing any data.

Editor or operational manager

Create it when that person will regularly maintain segments, visits, blocked periods, or boat settings.

Good practices

How to keep the workspace clean and useful

  • Define the season and the general route structure first. Then move into the detail.
  • Use consistent place names so the map, timeline, and visits are easy to read at a glance.
  • Block unavailable periods as early as possible. It is better to close a gap than to suggest unrealistic availability.
  • Create editors only when there is a clear maintenance responsibility.
  • If dates or route change, update the segments first and then review the affected visits and blocked periods.
Short flow to remember: boat → season → segments → visits → blocked periods → access. If you follow that order, the timeline stays coherent and the team works from one shared version of the plan.
